The 'Slack-to-Email' Migration: How to Route Non-Urgent Requests Out of Your Instant Messaging

If you feel like your workday is just a series of high-speed interruptions, you aren’t alone. For many of us, Slack, Microsoft Teams, or Google Chat has become a digital treadmill. Because the software is designed to mimic the feeling of sitting next to someone, we treat every message like a tap on the shoulder.

But not every request is a tap on the shoulder. Some are memos, some are project updates, and some are just “FYI” notes that don’t need a reply until tomorrow. When everything is urgent, nothing is. Here is how I systematically moved non-urgent requests out of my instant messaging apps and back into email—and how you can do the same without looking like you’re avoiding work.

Define the “Interrupt Threshold”

The first step is establishing a clear mental boundary for what belongs in an IM versus what belongs in an email. I use a simple rule: If the task requires me to leave my current “deep work” block to address it, it belongs in an email.

If a colleague asks, “Do you have the link for the marketing folder?” that is a sub-30-second task. I handle that in Slack. However, if they ask, “What do you think about the Q3 budget projections?” that requires me to open a spreadsheet, review data, and formulate a coherent thought. If that request comes through Slack, I ignore the urge to start working on it immediately. Instead, I reply: “That looks like a deep-dive request. Could you send that over via email so I can give it the attention it deserves when I get to my planning block?”

By calling it a “deep-dive request,” I’m not saying no. I’m framing the email as a way to prioritize the quality of the answer.

Update Your Status to Manage Expectations

Your colleagues aren’t trying to be annoying; they are just following the path of least resistance. You have to change the path. I use the custom status feature in Slack to signal my availability.

Don’t just put “In a meeting.” Use your status to set rules for communication. For example: “Focus block until 2:00 PM. If it’s not urgent, please email me.” When you have that status up, you’ve provided social permission for people to skip the ping and send an email instead. If someone pings you anyway, don’t feel guilty about letting the notification sit. If you respond immediately, you are training them to ignore your status.

The “Email Forward” Technique

Sometimes, a conversation starts in Slack and spirals out of control. It’s easy to get trapped in a long thread where you’re scrolling through 40 messages to find one piece of information.

When a discussion reaches the “this is getting complicated” stage, take the lead. Send a message like this: “I’m going to move this to email so we have a clear paper trail and I don’t lose track of these requirements. I’ll send a summary over shortly.”

Once you move it to email, you have total control. You can reply when you are ready, you can CC the right people, and you can archive the conversation when it’s done. You’ve successfully migrated a chaotic chat into a manageable work process.

Batch Your “Inbox Zero” Moments

The reason we hate email is that we treat it like a live chat, checking it every five minutes. To make the migration work, you have to treat email differently than you treat Slack.

  • Slack is for real-time: Keep it open, but keep notifications off except for direct mentions.
  • Email is for asynchronous work: Check it three times a day—start of the day, post-lunch, and 30 minutes before you sign off.

When you route things to email, you are essentially buying yourself time. You are moving from a state of reactive “ping-pong” to a state of managed response. If you get an email at 10:00 AM, you don’t need to answer it until your next designated email block. This prevents the “Slack fatigue” that comes from feeling like you have to be present in every conversation all at once.

How to Tell Your Team (Without Being Rude)

You don’t need to announce a “new policy.” People hate that. Instead, handle it conversationally. Next time someone sends you a multi-paragraph request via chat, respond with: “I want to make sure I don’t miss any of these details. Could you pop this into an email for me? It helps me keep my project files organized.”

Most people will be relieved. They are likely just as overwhelmed as you are, and they are usually grateful for a structure that keeps their own tasks organized as well. Framing it as a way for you to be more reliable makes the request sound like a favor to them, rather than a refusal to engage.

Moving non-urgent requests out of your instant messaging is the single most effective way to reclaim your focus. It turns your workday from a series of frantic reactions into a series of intentional tasks. Once you start shifting those complex requests into your inbox, you’ll find that you are more productive, your communication is clearer, and your stress levels at the end of the day are significantly lower.

Before you start shifting your workflow, it helps to consider how your current project management software might already support this transition. Take a moment to evaluate your current toolset and identify where you can start setting these new boundaries today.
