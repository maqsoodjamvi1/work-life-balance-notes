The 'Expectation Audit': How to Reset Unspoken Communication Norms With Your Manager

Most of us have a “phantom workload” that exists entirely outside of our official job description. It’s the string of Slack messages at 8:00 PM, the assumption that you’ll be available on your lunch break, or the expectation that a “quick question” email requires a response within fifteen minutes.

These aren’t usually policies written in an employee handbook. They are unspoken norms that grow like weeds in the gaps of our communication. If you feel like your work-life balance is slipping, it’s rarely because of your core responsibilities. It’s because these hidden expectations have expanded to fill every available hour.

An “Expectation Audit” is a deliberate process of pulling those unspoken norms into the light so you and your manager can agree on what is actually required—and what is optional.

Step 1: Track the “Hidden” Requests

For one week, keep a notepad or a simple digital document open. Every time you feel a spike of anxiety because of a communication, write it down.

Don’t just list your projects. List the mechanics of your work. For example:

  • “Received a Slack notification at 7:45 PM and felt I had to reply immediately.”
  • “Manager sent an email at 10:00 AM asking for a status update on a project that isn’t due until Friday.”
  • “Was expected to have my camera on for a meeting that was purely informational.”

At the end of the week, look for patterns. You will likely see that 80% of your stress comes from a specific medium (like chat apps) or a specific type of request (status updates vs. actual project work). This is your data set.

Step 2: The “Ask vs. Guess” Conversation

Many of us operate on a “guess culture.” We assume that because a manager sends an email late at night, they expect a response by morning. Often, they are just clearing their own inbox while their kids are finally asleep, and they have no intention of you working at that hour.

Schedule a one-on-one with your manager. Don’t frame this as a complaint about your workload. Frame it as an attempt to improve efficiency. Say something like: “I’ve been reviewing my workflow to make sure I’m hitting my priority deadlines. I’ve noticed some patterns in our communication that I want to clarify so I can be more effective.”

Walk them through your findings using these three categories:

  • The Response Window: “I’ve noticed a lot of messages come through after 6:00 PM. Are these intended for my immediate action, or should I treat these as items for the next business day?”
  • The “Quick Update” Loop: “When I get frequent requests for status updates, it breaks my flow on the deeper project work. Would you prefer a daily email summary at 4:00 PM instead of ad-hoc check-ins throughout the day?”
  • The Priority Filter: “I want to make sure I’m focusing on what matters most to you. When an urgent request comes in, how would you like me to weigh that against my current, scheduled tasks?”

Step 3: Setting “Guardrail” Norms

Once you’ve had the conversation, you need to turn these agreements into operational habits. This is the part where you take control of your time. If you told your manager you aren’t checking emails after 6:00 PM, you must stop checking them.

If you get a push notification on your phone, you have to be disciplined enough to ignore it until the morning. Your manager will eventually stop expecting an immediate response once they realize you aren’t going to provide one.

Use these tactics to reinforce the new boundaries:

  • The “Delayed Send” trick: If you work late because that’s your preferred schedule, use the “Schedule Send” feature to have your emails arrive in your manager’s inbox at 8:00 AM the next day. This prevents the habit of after-hours back-and-forth.
  • Status indicators: Use your Slack or Teams status to signal your focus. If you are doing deep work, set your status to “Focus Mode: Back at 2:00 PM.”
  • The “Yes, and…” technique: If a manager drops a new task on you when you’re already at capacity, don’t just say yes. Say: “I can certainly take that on. Given my current workload, which of these other two tasks would you like me to deprioritize so I can make this a priority?”

Why This Works

The reason this is effective is that it shifts the burden of communication from “mind-reading” to “agreement.” Most managers are juggling their own crises and aren’t consciously trying to sabotage your personal time. When you provide them with a clear, professional, and data-backed way to work with you, they usually welcome it. It makes them more efficient, too.

Remember, nobody is going to hand you your work-life balance on a silver platter. If you don’t define the parameters of your job, someone else will define them for you, and they will likely define them in a way that prioritizes their convenience over your well-being.

Once you have audited your expectations and had the conversation, your goal is to move into a maintenance phase. Keep tracking your flow, and don’t be afraid to revisit these norms during your quarterly or monthly reviews. Taking control is an iterative process, not a one-time event. Start by identifying the single biggest source of your “after-hours” stress today, and prepare your talking points for your next check-in.
